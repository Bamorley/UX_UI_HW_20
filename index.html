The Department of Labor (DOL) website provides information about workers' rights to safe and healthy working conditions, lawful compensation and overtime pay, employment discrimination laws, unemployment insurance, and more. Understandably, these topics can be difficult for people to understand and quickly digest.

Our goal during this project was to identify current pain paints with navigation and design and to create an intuitive site that is easy for users to navigate.

Research

Proto Persona

At the beginning of the research phase, we created a proto persona to help identify potential users and their needs when visiting the Department of Labor website. As we learned more about the website and its audience, we revised our persona to ensure Joe aligned with our target users.

Joe Kerman

35 years old

Behavioral Demographic

Education: Bachelors in electrical engineering

Career: Formerly an escape room engineer. Recently laid off because his company was struggling due to the COVID-19 pandemic. Even though things are reopening, escape rooms haven't bounced back.

Hobbies: Watching movies, playing board games, spending time with his family, and puzzling

Needs
  • Support his family financially
  • Learn if he qualifies for unemployment insurance and other unemployment benefits
  • Apply for unemployment insurance and determine who he should call or where he should go to start that process
Pain Points
  • He's confused by the US government website. They make it so difficult for citizens to acquire the goods and services they need.
  • Joe has never been jobless before so this process is entirely foreign to him. Where do you start?
  • He's anxious since his family doesn't have a ton of money saved up. He needs to figure out which benefits he qualifies for... and quickly.

Heuristic Evaluation

We performed a heuristic evaluation on the current site and identified five primary issues.

  • Content-dense pages that overwhelm users
  • Topics dropdown includes 40 links and link terminology is not always clear to users
  • Content on homepage doesn't represent what people are searching for
  • Not always clear when a link will take you to a third-party site
  • Design is consistent but white space isn't always used effectively
Definition

Problem Statement

Our goal for the Department of Labor website is to help users navigate the overwhelming amount of information and find answers to their employment questions. Users do not visit the Department of Labor website for fun or entertainment, so it's imperative that the website's organization is intuitive and easy to navigate for first-time visitors.

We observed that users are overwhelmed by the sheer volume of information on the Department of Labor website. They become frustrated since answers are spread across the site and content isn't always written in layman's terms.

How might we restructure the Department of Labor website to a provide a stress-free experience for users, in which they're able to navigate the site and quickly find the information they need?

Prioritization Matrix

After completing the research phase, we compiled user insights and data points into an affinity diagram. Then we created a prioritization matrix to identify high-level priorities.

Ideation

Card Sorting

On the current DOL site, the navigation bar includes 88 unique links, and 42 of those links are housed under "Topics." Users are forced to scan a long list of links to find what they're looking for.

To combat this problem, we sorted content into categories. Our intention is group content so that users can quickly scan the page and find what they're looking for.

Sitemap

We organized content into six primary categories:

  • For employees
  • For businesses
  • DOL agencies
  • Forms
  • About us
  • FAQ

Low-Fidelity Designs

Our low-fidelity designs provided the ground work for layout and homepage prioritization. The designs gave us room to add visual interest (icons, hero images, and color blocking) with future iterations.

Iterations

Style Guide

Our style guide was inspired by the current site, but we gave it a modern twist with updated iconography and green accent colors. The image samples focused on diversity and inclusion.

Iterations: Round One

Our first round of iterations included the following updates:

  • Darkened blue color for accessibility
  • Fixed navigation bugs
  • Adjusted margins and spacing
  • Moved bullet points from homepage cards
  • Added components and colors from the style guide

Iterations: Round Two

The most significant edit was changing “For Employees” to “For Individuals” in the navigation dropdown. User tests showed that “For Employees” was not always clear.

Some testers were confused why unemployment information was under an employees section (since you weren't technically employed anymore). One user thought that “For Employees” was specific to DOL employees.
